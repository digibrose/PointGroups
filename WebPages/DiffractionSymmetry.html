Point Groups part 3.

When a crystal diffracts X-rays the resulting rays form distinctive patterns on detectors. As the crystals are rotated these patterns change in systematic ways. Instead of thinking of the crystal rotating we can instead think of the detector rotating with the characteristic spots existing as points in space. The lattice of spots that this creates is known as the reciprocal lattice and exhibits symmetry closely related to that of the crystal itself. The difference however is that the diffraction pattern is centrosymetric. This means that any spot related by a rotation of 180° around the central point or origin has the same intensity. Because of this, extra symmetry elements are observed; namely mirror planes and inversion centres.

A mirror plane reflects an object through the plane whereas a point of inversion transforms objects through the central point inverting the object in the process. With an object like a sphere this will have no effect but with chiral objects like protein chains this will result in the mirror image of the protein, something that does not exist. It is for this reason that these symmetry elements are not observed in macromolecular crystallography. However because of the way diffraction occurs the lattice of diffracted rays always (nearly always) exhibit a centrosymmtric pattern meaning that the spot indexed 111 has the same intensity as -1-1-1 and 321 the same as -3-2-1. This means that alongside the symmetry observed in the crystals we now have these extra symmetries overlayed on the patterns.

No symmetry

When there is no symmetry observed in the crystal the resulting diffraction pattern shows only the symmetry resulting from the centrosymmetric nature of the diffraction pattern This is represented by a point of inversion through the center of the sphere on a stereographic projection. The nomenclature of this symmetry is -1.

Rotational Symmetry

The diffraction pattern symmetries associated with the simple rotations of even number combine those symmetries with that observed in -1 above. The result of this is to also create a mirror plane perpendicular to the rotation axis. the nomenclature for these point groups is 2/m, 4/m and 6/m.

This is not the case for trigonal symmetry because the inversion center doesn't create a perpendicular mirror. The reulsting point group is -3.

Dihedral Symmetry

Diffraction patterns of crystals exhibiting even dihedral symetry posses the symmetry of the the crystal alongside the symmetry created by the centrosymmetric nature of the diffraction. The result of this is to not only create a mirror perpendicular to rotational axis but to also create mirrors parallel to the primary axis.

Again this is slightly different for trigonal symmetry where the resulting pattern doesn't create the perpendicualr mirror plane but the parrallel planes are created.

Cubic Symmetry

The final point groups are the most complicated observed in macromolecular diffraction symmetry. They are however built up in exactly the same manner. Start with the symmetry elements of the crystallographic point group. Add a point of inversion and then the other symmetry operations appear as a result. It is worth noting that the symbol for a point of inversion occuring in the center of the sphere is repeated on all of the intersecting rotation axes apart from those on the equator. The nomeclature for these is again somewhat less clear with that resulting from diffraction from crsytals with 23 pointgroup having m-3 symmetry and those from 423 having m-3m.
