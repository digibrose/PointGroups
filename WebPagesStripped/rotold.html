Rotational Symmetry

Apart from no symmetry which has the point group number of 1, the pure rotation axes are probably the simplest symmetry elements to comprehend since the eye naturally recognises the degree of symmetry that results: The higher the order of the rotation axis, the more obvious to the eye is the symmetry. The definition of a symmetry rotation axis of order n is a line in space about which an object may be rotated anticlockwise by 360Â°/n such that its initial and final positions are indistinguishable.

The simplest and commonest example is that of two identical objects, perhaps a dimer of molecules. Such dimers often have a 2-fold axis and each monomer has an identical environment, with each "side" of the interface making the same interactions as the other as in the Yin and Yang Chinese symbol, shown below.

So why are these the only possible rotational symmetries observable in crystals? Why not 5 folds or 8 folds? It is simply because these symmetries do not form a repeatable lattice. A rotational symetry must move a lattice point to a succession of repeatable lattice points. This means if we look at the vectors between points related by rotational symmetry and transfer those vectors to the central point these vectors will point to the same lattice points. If we try this with other symmetries it is not the case. There are more complex proofs of this which we will not go into here.
